Most small business revenue forecasts are wrong. Not because forecasting is impossible, but because most forecasts are built on hope instead of data and process.

This playbook gives you a systematic approach to revenue forecasting that we use with CFO Line clients. It works for SaaS companies, professional services firms, and product businesses alike.

## Why Forecasting Matters

Before diving into the how, let's be clear about why accurate forecasting matters:

**Cash flow planning**: Knowing when revenue will arrive lets you time expenses and investments appropriately.

**Hiring decisions**: Revenue forecasts inform when you can afford to expand the team.

**Goal setting**: Realistic targets motivate teams; impossible ones demoralize.

**Investor communication**: If you have investors, they expect forecasts—and they remember when you miss.

## The Three Forecasting Methods

There's no single right way to forecast. The best approach combines multiple methods and compares results.

### Method 1: Bottom-Up (Pipeline-Based)

Start with what you know: your current sales pipeline.

**How it works:**
1. List every opportunity in your pipeline
2. Assign each a probability of closing (be honest)
3. Estimate the value if it closes
4. Multiply value × probability
5. Sum for total expected revenue

**Example:**
| Opportunity | Value | Probability | Expected |
|------------|-------|-------------|----------|
| Acme Corp | $50,000 | 80% | $40,000 |
| Beta LLC | $30,000 | 40% | $12,000 |
| Gamma Inc | $75,000 | 20% | $15,000 |
| **Total** | | | **$67,000** |

**Best for**: B2B businesses with defined sales cycles and CRM data.

**Watch out for**: Optimism bias. Most people overestimate close probabilities.

### Method 2: Top-Down (Historical Growth)

Use your historical performance to project forward.

**How it works:**
1. Calculate your average monthly growth rate over the past 6-12 months
2. Apply that rate to your current revenue
3. Adjust for known factors (seasonality, market changes, capacity)

**Example:**
- Current MRR: $100,000
- Average monthly growth (past 6 months): 8%
- Projected MRR next month: $108,000
- Projected MRR in 6 months: $158,687

**Best for**: Businesses with consistent historical performance and stable markets.

**Watch out for**: Past performance doesn't guarantee future results. Growth rates often slow as you scale.

### Method 3: Cohort Analysis

Track how customer groups behave over time.

**How it works:**
1. Group customers by when they signed up (cohorts)
2. Track revenue from each cohort over time
3. Identify patterns (expansion, contraction, churn)
4. Apply patterns to newer cohorts to project future revenue

**Example:**
- January cohort: Started at $10K MRR, now at $12K (20% expansion in 6 months)
- February cohort: Started at $15K MRR, now at $16.5K (10% expansion in 5 months)
- Apply similar expansion rates to newer cohorts

**Best for**: Subscription and recurring revenue businesses.

**Watch out for**: Requires good data hygiene and enough history to identify patterns.

## Building Your Forecast: Step by Step

### Step 1: Establish Your Baseline

Start with known revenue:
- Contracted recurring revenue
- Signed deals not yet started
- Renewal revenue (for subscription businesses)

This is your floor—the minimum you should expect.

### Step 2: Add Expected New Business

Use your pipeline to estimate new revenue:
- Apply realistic close rates
- Consider your historical conversion rates
- Account for typical sales cycle length

### Step 3: Account for Churn and Contraction

Don't forget the negatives:
- Expected customer churn (use historical rates)
- Downgrades and seat reductions
- Contract non-renewals

### Step 4: Layer in Expansion Revenue

Existing customers often grow:
- Upsells and cross-sells in pipeline
- Automatic expansion (seat-based pricing)
- Historical expansion rates

### Step 5: Build Three Scenarios

Never create just one forecast. Build:

**Conservative**: Only include high-probability revenue. Assume higher churn.

**Base case**: Your realistic expectation using median assumptions.

**Optimistic**: What happens if things go well. Higher close rates, lower churn.

### Step 6: Compare to Capacity

Reality-check your forecast against your capacity:
- Can your team deliver this much work?
- Do you have inventory/infrastructure?
- Is this growth rate achievable with current resources?

## Common Forecasting Mistakes

### 1. Forecasting in a vacuum
Don't just run numbers—talk to your sales team, account managers, and customers. Qualitative input catches things data misses.

### 2. Ignoring seasonality
Most businesses have seasonal patterns. Make sure your forecast accounts for them.

### 3. One-and-done forecasting
Good forecasting is iterative. Update weekly or monthly as new information arrives.

### 4. Over-precision
A forecast of "$127,458" implies false precision. Use ranges and scenarios instead.

### 5. Not tracking accuracy
If you don't compare forecasts to actuals, you can't improve. Track your accuracy over time.

## Putting It Into Practice

Start small:
1. Build a simple 3-month forecast using Method 1 or 2
2. Track accuracy each month
3. Refine your assumptions based on what you learn
4. Gradually extend to 6-12 month forecasts

Forecasting is a skill. You'll get better with practice, and even imperfect forecasts are better than no forecasts.
